Progress 0 of 24 tasks done
6+ months out
Get clear on your whyMindset
Write down your real reason for moving. Not the Instagram version — the honest one. This will carry you through the hard weeks. Ask: what am I moving toward, not just away from?
Research visa options for your destinationVisa & Legal
Look up visa categories: tourist, digital nomad, residence, work permit. Check official government immigration sites for your target country. Note income requirements, document lists, and processing times.
Build a 3-month emergency fundFinance
Aim for 3 months of living expenses saved before you go. This covers unexpected delays, a rough landing, or time to find your feet. Most expats who struggle early didn't have a buffer.
Decide what to do with your current homeLogistics
Sublet, end lease, or sell? If you own, consider renting it out. Give yourself enough notice time — most rental agreements require 1–3 months notice.
3–6 months out
Gather apostilled documentsVisa & Legal
Most countries need apostilled copies of your birth certificate, police clearance, marriage certificate (if applicable), and proof of income. An apostille is an internationally recognised authentication stamp. Processing can take weeks — start early.
Open a borderless bank accountFinance
Services like Wise or Revolut let you hold multiple currencies, pay abroad with low fees, and receive money internationally. Set this up before you leave — some require a home address to activate.
Consult an immigration lawyer or expat advisorVisa & Legal
For complex moves (family, assets, business), a one-hour paid consultation with an immigration specialist in your target country is worth every cent. Rules change frequently — don't rely solely on Facebook groups.
Research neighborhoods for your first landingLogistics
Don't lock into a long-term rental sight unseen. Book short-term housing for your first 4–6 weeks. Give yourself time to explore before committing.
Tell the people who matterMindset
Some conversations will be harder than others. Give people time to adjust. You don't need their permission, but rushed goodbyes create unnecessary grief. Plan your last visits and don't leave emotional loose ends.
1–3 months out
Submit visa applicationVisa & Legal
Submit with plenty of buffer time. Processing can take anywhere from 2 weeks to 3+ months depending on country and visa type. Keep copies of everything. Track your application reference number carefully.
Sort tax residency and obligationsFinance
Understand when you stop being tax-resident in your home country and what triggers obligations in your new one. Many countries use the 183-day rule. If you have assets, business income, or pensions — talk to a cross-border tax advisor.
Sort your health insuranceLogistics
Check if your home country insurance covers you abroad and for how long. Look into international health insurance (SafetyWing, Cigna, AXA are commonly used). Some visa applications require proof of coverage.
Cancel or redirect recurring bills and subscriptionsLogistics
Make a list: gym, subscriptions, local phone plan, car insurance, parking permits. Cancel or pause everything that won't work from abroad. Set a forwarding address for any physical mail.
Get an unlocked international phone planLogistics
Check if your phone is SIM-unlocked. Get a local SIM on arrival, or use an eSIM service like Airalo before you land. Having data from day one matters more than you'd think.
Final 4 weeks
Decide what to ship, store, or let goLogistics
Shipping internationally is expensive and slow. Most expats overpack and regret it. If you're not sure whether to bring something, leave it. You can replace most things abroad — sometimes for cheaper.
Do a "worst case" planning exerciseMindset
Write down: what happens if the visa is delayed? If housing falls through? If I feel really alone in month 2? Having pre-planned responses to likely hard scenarios calms anxiety and builds real confidence.
Set up a landing cash bufferFinance
Have access to cash or an easily accessible card from day one. ATMs abroad can be unreliable, and the first few days often involve fees, deposits, and setup costs you didn't plan for.
Check entry requirements for your arrival dateVisa & Legal
Rules change. Re-verify entry requirements within 2 weeks of your departure — passport validity (many countries require 6 months beyond your intended stay), onward ticket requirements, and any health documentation.
First week abroad
Register your address (if required)Logistics
Many countries require foreign nationals to register their address within 3–30 days of arrival. This is often the first step to getting a national ID number. Check requirements for your specific destination.
Find your nearest essential servicesLogistics
Locate: nearest pharmacy, supermarket, hospital or clinic, post office, and public transport stops. Walk your neighborhood in the first 48 hours.
Give yourself permission to feel all of itMindset
Excitement, grief, confusion, and awe often show up in the same day when you first land. That's normal. You just did something huge. Don't rush to perform "loving it" for social media. Let yourself land before you perform the landing.
